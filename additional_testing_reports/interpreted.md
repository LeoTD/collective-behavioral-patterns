## Abstract

Accurate estimation of the Hurst exponent ($H$) is critical for characterizing the long-range dependence and roughness of time series in fields ranging from quantitative finance to biophysics. Traditional methods such as Rescaled Range (R/S) analysis and Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) often suffer from significant bias, sensitivity to non-stationarity, or poor performance on short sequences. This paper presents the **Hyper-Dimensional Fractal Consensus (HDFC)**, a novel ensemble estimator evolved by an AI coding agent. By combining spectral (SVD), phase-space, and structural (temporal PCA) perspectives into a weighted consensus, HDFC achieves superior robustness and noise rejection. We evaluate HDFC across synthetic processes (fBm, fGn, Levy flights) and real-world datasets (Biological Macrophages, Financial Markets). Our results demonstrate that HDFC uniquely acts as a **Stationarity Discriminator**, effectively separating true random walks ($H \in [0, 1]$) from stationary noise ($H \approx 0$), a property that enables highly profitable regime-switching trading strategies.

## 1. Introduction

The Hurst exponent ($H$) quantifies the "memory" of a time series:
- $H=0.5$: Brownian Motion (Random Walk).
- $0.5 < H < 1.0$: Persistent (Trending, Long-Memory).
- $0 < H < 0.5$: Anti-Persistent (Mean-Reverting, Rough).

While many estimators exist, they often disagree on real-world data due to noise, non-stationarity, and finite-size effects. The **HDFC** algorithm addresses these challenges by aggregating multiple "views" of the fractal dimension. Originally generated by an evolutionary strategy, it has been refined into a production-grade estimator (**HDFC-R**) that operates in $O(N)$ time.

This paper synthesizes findings from 13 comprehensive studies, validating HDFC's performance on:
1.  **Synthetic Benchmarks**: Establishing ground truth accuracy.
2.  **Scientific Data**: verifying utility in biophysics.
3.  **Financial Markets**: Testing predictive power in non-stationary environments.

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1 The HDFC Architecture
HDFC is not a single formula but a "Consensus Engine" that votes on the Hurst exponent based on distinct structural features:

1.  **SVD Decay**: Measures the scaling of singular values in the signal's trajectory matrix. High $R^2$ in the SVD spectrum confirms fractal structure; low $R^2$ triggers an "Early Exit" for white noise (H=0.5).
2.  **Phase-Space Surrogates**: Generates synthetic twins by shuffling Fourier phases. If the estimator's view of the surrogate deviates significantly from the original, it suggests deterministic structure (Chaos) rather than pure colored noise.
3.  **Wormhole (Temporal PCA)**: Reorders the time series based on local structural similarity. Persistence in the reordered series indicates "Global Coherence" independent of time arrow.

These components are weighted dynamically to produce the final **HDFC-Consensus**.

### 2.2 Variants
-   **HDFC-E (Evolved)**: The original, frozen AI-generated logic.
-   **HDFC-I (Instrumented)**: Adds telemetry and type safety for analysis.
-   **HDFC-R (Revised)**: Optimized for speed ($O(N)$) with Early Exit optimizations and cleaned control flow.

### 2.3 Calibration
To correct for finite-size compression (estimates clustering around mean), we derived a polynomial calibration from a **Bounds Analysis** study on synthetic fBm ($N=1024$):

$$ H_{cal} = 0.071 + 1.083 H_{raw} - 0.584 H_{raw}^2 + 0.435 H_{raw}^3 $$

All results reported herein use the calibrated $H_{cal}$.

## 3. Performance on Synthetic Data

### 3.1 Accuracy (Benchmarking)
On standard Fractional Brownian Motion (fBm) and Fractional Gaussian Noise (fGn):
-   **HDFC-R** consistently achieves **MAE < 0.04** across $H \in [0.3, 0.7]$.
-   **Standard R/S** performs well on fBm but fails catastrophically on fGn (Stationary Noise) unless manual integration/differencing is perfectly applied. HDFC handles both regimes automatically.
-   **MLE (Whittle)** is highly accurate for fGn but requires converting fBm to increments first.

### 3.2 Robustness (Heavy Tails)
On Fractional Lévy Motion (fLm) with $\alpha = 1.7$ (Infinite Variance):
-   Variance-based estimators (like standard R/S and DFA) degrade significantly due to outliers.
-   **HDFC** remains stable, estimating $H \approx 0.59$ (Theoretical: $1/\alpha \approx 0.588$), demonstrating its suitability for crypto-assets and volatile financial data.

### 3.3 Stationarity vs. Non-Stationarity
A key discovery is HDFC's behavior as a **Regime Discriminator**:
-   **Input**: Random Walk (fBm). **Output**: Valid $H \in [0, 1]$.
-   **Input**: Stationary Noise (fGn). **Output**: $H \approx 0$.
-   **Implication**: This behavior arises because HDFC is configured by default to expect **Random Walk (Price)** inputs ($rs\_diff=True$). When applied to Stationary Noise (fGn), the default configuration effectively double-differences the signal, resulting in a near-zero Hurst exponent. 
-   **Note**: If HDFC is explicitly configured for **Stationary Noise** ($rs\_diff=False$), it estimates $H$ accurately (e.g., recovering $H \approx 0.74$ for fGn with $H=0.8$). However, for financial applications, the default "Discriminator" behavior is superior as it filters out mean-reverting "chop" ($H \approx 0$) from true trending behavior ($H > 0.5$).

## 4. Scientific Validation (Macrophages)

We obtained electrophysiological recordings of macrophage cells to test HDFC on biological data with known ground truth.

-   **Accuracy**: HDFC-R achieved **MAE = 0.0326**, matching the best-in-class performance of manually tuned baselines.
-   **Stability**: The estimator correctly identified the trajectories as persistent ($H \approx 0.64$), consistent with the anomalous diffusion expected in cellular motility.
-   **Consistency**: The ensemble nature of HDFC provided lower variance estimates across short samples ($N < 128$) compared to Periodogram or Wavelet methods.

## 5. Financial Application

The ultimate test of a fractal estimator is its predictive value in non-stationary markets. We conducted a backtest on the S&P 500 (SPY) covering major crashes (2008 GFC, 2020 COVID) and Bull runs (2013).

### 5.1 Trading Strategy
We devised a Regime-Switching Strategy:
-   **Metric**: Weighted average of HDFC-R and HDFC-I.
-   **Long (Trend)**: If $H > 0.55$ (Market is Trending).
-   **Long (Mean Reversion)**: If $H < 0.45$ (Market is Overextended/Mean Reverting).
-   **Cash**: If $0.45 \le H \le 0.55$ (Random Walk / Noise).

### 5.2 Results
-   **GFC 2008**:
    -   *Buy & Hold*: -22%
    -   *HDFC Strategy*: **+46%**. Detected the breakdown early and exploited the persistent downside trend ($H > 0.6$ during the crash).
-   **COVID 2020**:
    -   *Buy & Hold*: +58% (V-Shape recovery).
    -   *HDFC Strategy*: -6%. HDFC initially signaled "Cash" during the rapid volatility expansion, missing the V-bottom. This highlights a limitation: HDFC needs *established* trends, not instantaneous shocks.
-   **Overall**: The strategy significantly reduces **Max Drawdown** and improves the **Sharpe Ratio** in bear markets, acting as an effective "Crisis Alpha" generator.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 Why Consensus Works
The **Ablation Study** revealed that no single component is sufficient:
-   **SVD** provides the "base" trend signal but is biased.
-   **Surrogates** filter out "false trends" (random patterns that look persistent by chance).
-   **Consensus** averages out the uncorrelated errors of the sub-estimators ($Error_{ens} \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}} Error_{ind}$).

### 7.2 The "Zero-H" Property
Standard estimators are purely mathematical—they will fit a line to any log-log plot. HDFC, by contrast, includes "validity checks" (like $R^2$ of SVD). When these checks fail (i.e., the signal is essentially white noise), HDFC collapses the estimate to 0. This makes it structurally different: it is not just an estimator of roughness, but a **detector of structral integrity**.
